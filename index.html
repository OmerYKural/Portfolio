Research and technical profile

Lighthouse Biophysics Research Fellowship

The Lighthouse Biophysics Research Fellowship is a selective, multi-year extracurricular program centered on biophysics, quantitative biology, microscopy, engineering design, and computational analysis. Its structure matches the kind of work I am most drawn to: long-horizon, technically demanding, and grounded in responsibility.

My background aligns naturally with that environment. I have spent years working with 3D CAD and 3D printing, including maintaining and troubleshooting my own Ender 3 V2, designing models, and selecting materials based on performance needs. I understand that technical work is rarely flawless on the first pass; it requires patience, persistence, and an ability to diagnose systems rather than simply use them.

Beyond fabrication, I have had direct exposure to research environments through lab visits, imaging work, and an intensive period working with cultured breast cancer cells and a high-end research microscope. I have also been exploring the computational side of scientific work, including basic Python, stronger experience in JavaScript, and early work toward an AI-assisted imaging project inspired by microscopy data.

Why this path fits me

I am especially interested in work that does not split theory from practice. The most compelling problems, to me, are the ones that require both careful reasoning and actual building: instruments, workflows, analysis pipelines, or systems that must function under real constraints.

That is why my long-term goals converge around Mechanical Engineering, Biochemistry, and Neuroscience. Engineering trains precision and systems thinking. Biochemistry brings molecular depth. Neuroscience opens a path toward understanding complexity in living systems from a human-centered perspective.

Together, those interests support the kind of future I want: one where I can contribute to technical projects with real scientific weight, while continuing to develop as both a builder and a thinker.

Independent technical problem-solving

One example that reflects how I think came when our dishwasher failed at home. Rather than stop at frustration, I investigated the issue, opened the unit carefully, identified a broken connection, and used a soldering iron to repair it. The machine worked again afterward.

What mattered most was not only the fix itself, but the lesson: strong technical work is not just boldness; it also requires efficient troubleshooting, model-specific research, and knowing when to move from exploration to method.

Research-aligned fabrication and computing

My experience with 3D printing and CAD is not superficial hobby exposure. It includes repeated troubleshooting, replacement-part fabrication, materials judgment, and a strong grasp of how machines behave over time.

Combined with interest in scientific imaging and early AI ideas for microscopy-related workflows, that background gives me a practical entry point into biophysics research and engineering-heavy scientific environments.
